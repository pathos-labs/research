Executive Summary

Five Key Findings

01 Veterans are uniquely positioned to solve the industry’s retention crisis. Their tolerance for sustained discomfort directly combats the #1 reason rookies wash out.
02 Interview weaknesses are documented and fixable. Translation gap, jargon, nervousness — behavioral, not competency-based.
03 Multiple firms have veteran pipelines but none publish comparative retention or AUM data. The programs exist; the proof is anecdotal.
04 The American College is the most important institutional player — full scholarships for CFP®, ChFC®, CLU® + mentorship.
05 SkillBridge is underutilized. 70%+ interest but only 32% participation. 58% of installations lack sufficient partnerships.

Specific Mechanisms

Why Military Backgrounds Predict Success

01 Prospecting Tolerance Trained tolerance for sustained discomfort pursuing long-term objectives. The #1 reason rookies fail is the cold prospecting grind.
02 OODA Loop Decisions Observe-Orient-Decide-Act under uncertainty. Advising requires confident recommendations with incomplete information.
03 Contingency Planning Military planning always includes “what if” scenarios. Financial planning is fundamentally the same discipline.
04 Trust-Building Service record signals trustworthiness. 72% of investors associate military experience with integrity.
05 Structured Risk Assessment “Trained in structured approaches to risk management where people and technology work together.” — Brad Genser, ex-Goldman Sachs
06 Accountability Culture Military consequences are real. This translates to fiduciary mindset — personal responsibility for client outcomes.

Section 02

The Translation Gap

Veterans’ weaknesses are behavioral, not competency-based — which means they’re fixable

02
FIMT-RC, 2024 · Peer-Reviewed

Known Gaps, Documented Fixes

14 HR professionals + 93 veterans surveyed. Both groups converge on the same strengths and weaknesses.

Strengths

  • ✓ Communication of soft skills
  • ✓ Confidence
  • ✓ Professionalism

Weaknesses

  • ✗ Translating military skills to civilian language
  • ✗ Use of military jargon
  • ✗ Nervousness

Veterans who completed interview training were more likely to find jobs (Perkins 2022) and leave for better jobs (Morgan 2022).

The Jargon Problem

From Military to Civilian Language

“I commanded a company of 120 personnel” → “I led a team of 120 people through complex operations”
“Managed a $15M MTOE” → “I was responsible for a $15 million equipment and logistics portfolio”
“Served as S-3 in a BCT” → “I was the chief operations officer for a 4,000-person organization”
“Executed FRAGOs under time constraints” → “I developed and communicated revised action plans under tight deadlines”

The fix: Lead with the result, then provide context only if asked. Veterans brief bottom-up; interviews want the conclusion first.

First-Year Risks

Where Veterans Struggle

  • ▸ The Prospecting Wall — 75% of all rookies fail within 3 years. Veterans who expect structure rather than self-generated business hit this hardest.
  • ▸ Income Shock — Commission-based early compensation vs. steady military pay + benefits. Younger veterans lack the pension cushion.
  • ▸ Role Misalignment — A veteran expecting “financial planning” may spend 80% of time prospecting. Clear expectations setting is critical.
  • ▸ Cultural Translation — Rigid posture, brief answers, “Sir/Ma’am” can create distance with some client demographics.
  • ▸ Isolation — Military life is communal. Solo-advisor models feel isolating. Mentorship and veteran ERGs “significantly reduce first-year attrition.”

Hire Heroes USA · FIMT-RC 2024

Section 06

What We Don’t Know

Research gaps, counter-arguments, and the limits of the evidence

06
Intellectual Honesty

Critical Research Gaps

01 No comparative performance data — No published study compares veteran vs. non-veteran advisor AUM, retention, or satisfaction.
02 No veteran-specific FINRA exam pass rates. Military study habits likely help, but we have zero data.
03 Firm programs may be partly performative. Many “veteran programs” are marketing tools without published outcomes data.
04 Veterans face financial literacy challenges themselves — only 20% of military test-takers passed a financial knowledge quiz vs. 41% of civilians.
