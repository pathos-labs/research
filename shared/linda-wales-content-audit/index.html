LinkedIn's algorithm is structurally working against corporate content

This is not a content quality problem. LinkedIn's platform architecture has shifted decisively toward amplifying individuals over brands. Three data points make this concrete:

Company pages now reach only 1.6% of their followers. That is a 15% drop from late 2023, and it is getting worse. Company content now occupies just 2% of the total LinkedIn feed, down from 7% in 2021.[1]Richard van der Blom (2025). Algorithm InSights Report 2025. Just Connecting. Analysis of 1.8M posts.

Personal profiles receive 561% more reach than company pages sharing identical content. In a normalised study, employee profiles generated 2.75x more impressions and 5x more engagement per 1,000 followers than the company page. The top-performing employee generated 72 times the normalised impressions of the brand page.[2]Refine Labs (2025). "Personal LinkedIn Engagement vs Company Page." Multi-quarter study normalised per 1,000 followers.

Reposts are actively suppressed. When you hit "repost" on corporate content, the algorithm recognises it as duplicate and gives it 4 to 10 times less reach than an original post on the same topic. Employee reposts of company content lose approximately 30% of their impact versus original posts.[1]Richard van der Blom (2025). Algorithm InSights Report 2025. Cited by ThinkLikeAPublisher. The 30% figure is from van der Blom's dataset.

What the algorithm now rewards instead

LinkedIn deployed a single 150-billion-parameter AI model called "360Brew" in 2025, replacing thousands of specialised models. The key shifts: expertise over virality (substantial, industry-relevant content gets 124% more distribution; content-empty viral posts lose 76% of reach), saves as the key metric (bookmarkable content gets disproportionately boosted), and AI-generated content penalised (30% less reach, 55% less engagement versus human-written posts). Comments over 15 words boost reach 2x versus short comments. The algorithm increasingly rewards "expertise signals" — comments using industry-specific terminology, shares from people with relevant job titles, and dwell time correlated with content depth.

The algorithm changes frequently. These mechanics may shift within 6-12 months. But the structural preference for individuals over company pages has been consistent for 5+ years and shows no sign of reversing.

The Edelman-LinkedIn research found that 90% of decision-makers are more receptive to sales outreach from people who consistently produce quality thought leadership. 75% researched products they had not previously considered because of a piece of thought leadership. 60% said good thought leadership makes them willing to pay a premium.[4]Edelman-LinkedIn (2024). B2B Thought Leadership Impact Report. n=3,484.

And perhaps most relevantly for your accounts: 70% of C-suite leaders said a piece of thought leadership led them to question whether they should continue working with an existing supplier.[4]Edelman-LinkedIn (2024). 25% said it led them to ending or significantly reducing that relationship.

The "hidden buyers" who evaluate you through content

The 2025 Edelman-LinkedIn report specifically studied "hidden buyers" — internal stakeholders who influence purchases but are not the primary user. These are the people inside the Home Office and Parliament who shape buying decisions behind the scenes. 79% said they are more likely to support proposals from vendors whose employees publish strong thought leadership. 53% said when thought leadership is strong, brand recognition matters less. These hidden buyers are less likely to take sales meetings — they evaluate through content. If your content is corporate reposts, they see nothing. If your content is genuine JES domain expertise, they see someone worth backing internally.

The peer-reviewed evidence: personal content drives revenue, promotional content does not

The only peer-reviewed study directly measuring LinkedIn content's impact on sales revenue — Mora Cortez, Johnston & Ghosh Dastidar (2023) in the Journal of Business Research, using a vector autoregression model on 106 weeks of data — found that "social" posts (relationship-building, non-promotional) positively affect sales revenue, while sales-oriented posts drive follower growth but not revenue. A companion study (2022, 68 citations) found that "sincerity" and "excitement" personality dimensions drive B2B engagement, while "competence" — the default register of corporate content — does not.

The deals forming around you are invisible — and corporate content never enters the room

70% of B2B purchasing decisions are substantially complete before a buyer contacts a seller.[5]6sense (2024). Buyer Experience Report. Consistent with Gartner and Forrester research. 80% of vendor shortlists are finalised before direct sales contact. But where are those opinions forming?

In private channels you cannot see. 77 to 84% of content sharing happens through DMs, email, Slack, WhatsApp, and Teams — channels that are invisible to any analytics dashboard.[6]Multiple sources: Cognism, StrategicABM, Intent Amplify. Consistent with the RadiumOne dark social study. Exact percentages vary by study. Your government accounts involve 14 to 23 stakeholders building consensus, sharing content through exactly these private channels.

The content that gets forwarded in private channels has specific characteristics: a strong point of view, specific data or frameworks, honesty about limitations, applicability to the reader's situation. Corporate marketing content does not get forwarded. Nobody DMs a colleague a Splunk observability blog post. They do DM a post where a named expert — someone with genuine credentials in their space — shares an insight they had not considered.

Your Digital Policing Summit post — where you wrote "as a consumer of these services" — is the kind of content that gets forwarded in a Home Office Slack channel. Your Splunk AI reshare is not. The engagement numbers you can see are the tip of the iceberg. The sharing you cannot see is where the pipeline forms.

Three questions to ask before every post

If the answer to any of these is wrong, do not post it from your personal profile.

  1. 1
    Does this build the reputation I want in five years, or someone else's pipeline?
    Your techUK JES Council role, your Digital Policing Summit posts, your Parish Council work — these build toward a future where you are known as one of the most credible voices in justice and emergency services technology. A Splunk observability repost builds toward nothing. Ask where this post is taking you.
  2. 2
    Would someone read this if they didn't know my employer?
    Your post about the Digital Policing Summit would be interesting regardless of where you work. It reflects genuine domain knowledge and honest enthusiasm. A Splunk product update is interesting only to people already in your employer's sales funnel — and they are getting it from Splunk's own channels already. If the content has no value without the corporate context, it is marketing, not thought leadership.
  3. 3
    Could only I share this insight, or could anyone at my company?
    You have two years on the techUK JES Management Committee, accounts covering Law Enforcement and Parliament, a Parish Council seat, and a genuine passion for policing outcomes that shows in your writing. Nobody else at Splunk has that combination. When you post from that intersection — technology and justice, from someone who understands both the policy and the product — you are differentiated. When you share generic product content, you are interchangeable.

If all three answers point to you — your reputation, your standalone value, your unique perspective — post it. If any answer points to your employer, consider whether you can write your own commentary on the underlying topic instead. Fifteen minutes of original writing will outperform a repost by an order of magnitude, because the algorithm, the audience, and the trust dynamics are all aligned in favour of your voice.

The evidence behind these three questions

Question 1 draws on Dorie Clark's "Long Game" framework (2021, Harvard Business Review contributor, Duke Fuqua) — the principle that real thought leadership requires treating personal brand as a multi-year investment. Question 2 adapts the "Helpful vs. Promotional" test from practitioner Matt Homewood (Jam7, 2026), originally grounded in the Edelman-LinkedIn research showing buyers can tell the difference. Question 3 draws on Chris Latham's Expertise Audit (2025): "Look at your last 20 posts. If fewer than 15 demonstrate specific expertise only you could share, you have found the problem." The underlying principle — original, expertise-driven content outperforms corporate amplification — is confirmed by all five data sources in the evidence section below.

What this looks like: two posts a week, written as yourself

This is not about posting more. It is about posting differently. Two to three original posts per week — drawing on your genuine expertise in justice technology, your experience at the Digital Policing Summit, your perspective from the JES Council, your view from Penrith on how government works at every level — will outperform seven corporate reposts.

Only 15% of decision-makers rate available thought leadership as "very good or excellent."[4]Edelman-LinkedIn (2024). Less than half (48%) rate it as "good." The quality bar is low. You clear it every time you write as yourself about policing transformation or digital justice. The competitive moat is not volume — it is substance.

The corporate reposts do not need to be confrontational. You do not need to stop engaging with Splunk's content entirely. But the next time you are about to hit "repost," try this instead: take the underlying topic — observability in government, resilience in critical infrastructure, AI in policing — and write three to five sentences of your own take. What have you seen in your accounts that confirms or contradicts the marketing claim? What do your clients actually care about? That is the post your audience is waiting for.

The timeline: what to expect and when

Thought leadership does not produce pipeline overnight. The practitioner evidence converges on a consistent timeline: Months 1-2: engagement metrics only (views, shares, comments). Months 3-6: early pipeline signals, peers start engaging, you begin to be seen as a voice in your space. Months 6-9: steady inbound enquiries directly traced to content. Month 12+: compounding returns, speaking and collaboration opportunities, recognition as a domain authority. The Edelman data adds a concrete milestone: 86% of decision-makers say they are somewhat or very likely to invite consistent, quality thought leadership producers to participate in RFPs. This is the long game. Two posts a week for six months. That is the investment.

One more thing: honesty is your sharpest edge

The Pratfall Effect (Aronson, 1966, well-replicated in social psychology) shows that admitting minor limitations makes competent people more credible, not less. Applied to B2B content: "When you are transparent about where your solution is not ideal, competent buyers trust your claims about where it excels." Most thought leadership fails because it sounds like marketing — all upside, no honesty. When you write about what digital policing still gets wrong, or what government tech procurement misunderstands about resilience, you are doing something almost nobody in enterprise sales does. That is the content that earns trust, gets forwarded in private channels, and eventually earns the meeting.
